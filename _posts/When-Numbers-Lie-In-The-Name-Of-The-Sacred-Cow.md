## National Statistics Day: A Reminder That Numbers Are Sacred

Today, June 29, is National Statistics Day in India — observed annually to honour the birth anniversary of Prof. Prasanta Chandra Mahalanobis, the visionary statistician who founded the Indian Statistical Institute and laid the foundations of India's national data infrastructure. Mahalanobis understood something that too many researchers today seem to have forgotten: that a number carries a moral weight. It either reflects reality, or it does not. There is no middle ground.

In Ayurveda, this principle has ancient roots. Our tradition did not merely observe — it counted, classified, and systematised. The enumeration of Doshas, Dhatus, and Srotas; the precise specifications of Panchakarma procedures; the careful grading of Bala and Agni — all of these are acts of rigorous quantification embedded in the epistemological framework of a tradition that understood the difference between observation and assertion.

It is therefore both fitting and deeply troubling that on this day — National Statistics Day — I find myself writing about a government-funded research programme in which the numbers, quite simply, do not add up.

In Ayurveda, we cannot afford to play with numbers. Our credibility as a system of medicine depends on the integrity of the data we generate. When we fabricate, inflate, or manipulate figures — even in the name of validating traditional knowledge — we are not serving the tradition. We are betraying it.

*Mahalanobis taught India that data is the foundation of policy. What happens when the data itself is built on sand — and public policy, and public funds, rest upon it?*

## What Is SUTRA-PIC and What Did It Cost?

Launched in April 2020 by the Department of Science and Technology, the SUTRA-PIC programme — Scientific Utilization Through Research Augmentation, Prime Products from Indigenous Cows — was India's first dedicated grant programme for research on indigenous cow breeds and their byproducts: milk, urine, and dung. Ten projects were sanctioned. The total approved outlay: Rs. 98 crore of public funds.

Four of these projects were led by researchers at IITs — among India's most prestigious technical institutions. The findings from three of these projects have now been published in indexed international journals. A fourth study on panchgavya awaits publication after a six-month extension was sought.

On the surface, this looks like science. It has the institutions, the journals, the DOI numbers. But as a detailed post-publication analysis by Dr. Cyriac Abby Philips (TheLiverDoc) has now established — and as three major international publishers have formally confirmed they are investigating — what lies beneath that surface is deeply disturbing.

*The question I am asking today is not whether cows have value. They do — cultural, agricultural, ecological, and perhaps pharmacological. The question is whether this is how Rs. 98 crore of taxpayer money should be spent, and whether the science produced in return meets even the minimum standards of integrity.*

## The Post-Publication Reckoning: What TheLiverDoc Found — and What Publishers Confirmed

Dr. Cyriac Abby Philips, a hepatologist and clinical researcher known publicly as TheLiverDoc, conducted a systematic, paper-by-paper post-publication analysis of the SUTRA-PIC publications and formally wrote to the publishers of each journal. His findings — now acknowledged and under formal investigation by Springer Nature, ACS Publications, and Elsevier — constitute one of the most serious scientific integrity challenges to emerge from any government-funded Indian research programme in recent memory.

Let me go through each paper in turn.

## Paper One — The IIT-BHU (Varanasi) Paper

*Metabolomic Profiling of Cow Urine of Various Breeds Reveals Bioactive Metabolites of Diverse Industrial Applications* — Kushwaha, Singh, Mahesh, Yadav, Sheth, Dhoble. *Applied Biochemistry and Biotechnology*, July 2025. DOI: 10.1007/s12010-025-05300-6.

The study collected urine from eight cow breeds — seven indigenous Indian breeds and one exotic Jersey breed — and ran GC-MS chemical analysis, claiming to identify bioactive compounds with pharmaceutical and industrial applications. Dr. Philips's post-publication analysis identified five categories of serious problems.

**Contaminants reported as cow compounds.** The researchers reported common laboratory contaminants as genuine metabolites of cow urine. Crucially, they never ran blank control tests — the most elementary step in any GC-MS study — which would have immediately exposed the contamination. This is not a sophisticated error. Blank controls are taught in undergraduate analytical chemistry.

**Chemicals listed that cannot exist in urine.** The paper lists compounds that are physically impossible in animal urine: toxic synthetic metal compounds, a banned pesticide, prescription-only drugs, and at least one wax-like molecule so heavy it could not pass through their own instrument. These were reported as analytical findings without comment.

**Health claims for compounds never detected.** The paper credits cow urine with antioxidant, antimicrobial, and anticancer properties — citing approximately twenty named compounds as responsible. These compounds appear nowhere in their own data tables. The claims and the evidence are from entirely different universes.

**Discussion that directly contradicts the data tables.** The authors describe certain compounds as 'breed-specific' — a finding that would have genuine biological interest. Their own data tables show these same compounds appearing uniformly across every breed. The contradiction is not subtle.

**A broken reference list.** Citations are repeated illogically, several point to unrelated plant studies, and — most tellingly — at least one paper documenting a chemical's toxic effects is cited as evidence that the chemical is beneficial.

The publisher's response: **Springer Nature Research Integrity Group** confirmed in writing to Dr. Philips that the concerns are taken seriously and will be investigated in accordance with COPE guidelines.

*This is not a paper that made methodological errors under pressure. This is a paper that appears to have reported things that were not there, and failed to check for things that would have invalidated everything.*

## Paper Two — The IIT-Roorkee Paper

*Deciphering the Pharmacological Constituents of Cow Urine Distillate Responsible for Antiviral Activity against Chikungunya Virus* — Saha, Handa, Kumar, V. Singh, V.A. Singh, Mitra, Saini, A. Kumar, S. Singh, Sircar, P. Kumar, Tomar. *ACS Agricultural Science & Technology*, Vol. 6, Issue 6, May 28, 2026.

This is the paper that first attracted widespread attention. The researchers tested a distillate of cow urine against Chikungunya virus in a cell culture and reported up to 99.9% viral reduction. Three compounds — benzoic acid, hippuric acid, and oleic acid — were claimed to block a key viral enzyme. Dr. Philips's analysis identified five categories of integrity problems.

**An identical result in two independent experiments.** Two completely different experimental setups both produced a kill rate of exactly 99.85%. In genuine independent experiments, this is statistically near-impossible. It does not happen by chance. It raises a direct and serious question of data fabrication.

**A synthetic human contraceptive hormone 'found' in cow urine.** The chemical analysis 'detected' medroxyprogesterone — a wholly synthetic hormone used only in human contraceptives. It does not occur naturally in any animal. Its presence in the dataset means either the sample was contaminated or the chemical identifications cannot be trusted. The paper made no note of this whatsoever.

**The synergy threshold was quietly redefined.** The accepted scientific threshold for demonstrating drug synergy is a combination index of 0.5 or lower. The researchers' result was 0.73 — which constitutes no real synergistic effect. Rather than report this as a negative finding, the paper quietly redefined the threshold to make a failure appear as a success.

**The confirmatory second sample contradicts the first.** The researchers claimed a second cow urine product gave 'similar results.' This second product was missing hippuric acid entirely — one of the three compounds the paper argues is responsible for the entire antiviral mechanism. The two products are chemically unlike. 'Similar results' is not an accurate description of what the data shows.

**The virus may simply have been killed by poisoned cells.** The distillate was tested at concentrations high enough to kill up to one-third of the host cells. At such doses, the apparent reduction in virus is likely a consequence of cell death, not genuine antiviral action. Correcting for cytotoxicity is a fundamental methodological requirement in any antiviral assay. It was not done.

The publisher's response: **William King, Senior Managing Editor, ACS Publications**, confirmed in writing that ACS Agricultural Science & Technology takes these concerns seriously, follows COPE guidance, and will move forward with their standard investigative process.

*This is not merely an in vitro study with modest limitations. This is an in vitro study with data that appears fabricated, chemical identifications that appear unreliable, and statistical thresholds that appear to have been moved post hoc to manufacture a positive result.*

## Paper Three — The IIT-Dhanbad Paper

*Cr(VI)-captured polyaniline-coated carbon derived from cow dung for sustainable heavy metal remediation and high-performance supercapacitor applications* — Sonal, Acharya, Mohanta, Tripathi, Nayak, Mishra. *Biochemical Engineering Journal*, Elsevier.

This paper sits at a slight remove from direct health claims — it is an environmental engineering and materials science study, claiming a cow dung-derived material can clean chromium from polluted water and then serve as an energy storage electrode. The integrity problems are, if anything, numerically the starkest of all three papers.

**They measured the wrong thing.** The paper claims the material captured toxic chromium from water. What they actually measured was whether chromium disappeared from the water. Their own data shows the chromium merely changed chemical form and remained dissolved — it was not captured. The paper's central claim is contradicted by the paper's own data.

**The material barely contains chromium.** When the spent material was directly measured, it held roughly sixty times less chromium than the headline adsorption capacity requires. The numbers do not reconcile with each other.

**Four samples report identical error values to three decimal places.** In real independent experimental measurements, this cannot happen. Measurement error is never identical across independent samples to three decimal places. This statistical pattern is a well-recognised hallmark of fabricated or copied data.

**The same material is assigned two wildly different chromium contents.** One figure in the paper reports 0.09% chromium. Another reports 4.24% — a 47-fold discrepancy in the same material. The paper does not address or acknowledge this contradiction.

**Energy storage efficiency is reported above 100%.** This is physically impossible. Energy storage systems cannot exceed 100% efficiency. The paper reports this value without comment or correction.

**The sample count is inconsistent.** At different points in the paper, 12, 14, and 17 samples are described as having been tested. These are not rounding differences. They are three different numbers for the same experiment.

The publisher's response: **Nathanial Antony J C, Senior Journal Manager, Elsevier Journal Operations**, confirmed in writing that the concerns have been forwarded to the Editor and Publisher of the Biochemical Engineering Journal and will be investigated.

*Four samples with identical error values to three decimal places. Energy efficiency above 100%. A 47-fold discrepancy in the same material. Two different sample counts in the same paper. These are not the signatures of sloppy science. They are the signatures of data that was not collected — but composed.*

## The Uncomfortable Questions That Must Be Asked

**Why were IITs given this mandate at all?** India has dedicated Ayurveda research institutions — CCRAS, AIIA, NIA, and a network of postgraduate Ayurveda colleges with laboratories and stated research mandates. The SUTRA-PIC funds went to engineering departments at IITs. The reason, I would argue, is institutional prestige laundering: a finding from an IIT letterhead travels further in public discourse and gets published in higher-ranking journals than the same finding from an Ayurveda college. The IIT brand does not validate the premise. It manufactures the appearance of validation.

**Why is there no clinical research?** Ayurveda's therapeutic claims are claims about outcomes in human patients. Not one of these papers addresses a clinical question. We have in vitro assays, metabolomics profiles, and materials science experiments. None of this tells us whether any Ayurvedic intervention is effective or safe in a living human being. This is not scientific validation of Ayurveda. It is scientific window dressing.

**What does an in vitro antiviral study actually prove?** The IIT-Roorkee Chikungunya paper — setting aside its integrity problems entirely — was at best a preliminary cell culture experiment. An in vitro finding is the first step in a research pipeline that requires years of animal studies, toxicology, and clinical trials before any therapeutic claim can be responsibly made. Yet this study was funded under a programme explicitly aimed at 'validation' and 'commercialization.' The translational gap between a monkey kidney cell culture and a human patient is vast. It should not be crossed in a press release.

**Where were the blank controls?** The IIT-BHU metabolomics paper reported laboratory contaminants as cow urine metabolites because blank tests were not run. This is not a sophisticated methodological debate. Blank controls are taught in the first year of undergraduate chemistry. Their absence in a Rs. 98 crore government programme is not an oversight. It is a systems failure.

**Who reviewed these papers before publication?** Peer review failed here — visibly and comprehensively. Three papers with these levels of internal contradiction, impossible data, and fabrication signatures passed through the editorial processes of three major international publishers. This reflects a broader crisis in academic publishing. But it also reflects what happens when ideologically motivated research generates attractive results: reviewers who want to believe the conclusion are less likely to interrogate the data.

**Who is accountable for the Rs. 98 crore?** This is taxpayer money. It was spent on a programme with a predetermined cultural conclusion — that indigenous cow products are beneficial — and deployed scientific infrastructure to generate supporting data. The data, in at least three published cases, appears to be unreliable, partially fabricated, or wholly manufactured. Who in the Government of India is asking what was actually produced for this investment? Who in the DST is conducting an internal audit? Who in the Ayush Ministry is examining what 'scientific validation' actually means in practice?

*On National Statistics Day, the most important question is this: when the numbers in a government-funded study are not real, who is responsible? And how many more papers from this programme are waiting to be examined?*

## What Ayurveda Actually Needs

I am an Ayurvedic physician. I have spent over three decades in this tradition as a clinician, surgeon, and educator. I am not writing this to discredit Ayurveda. I am writing this because I am convinced that ideologically motivated, methodologically compromised research — laundered through prestigious institutions and international journals — is the single greatest threat to Ayurveda's long-term credibility.

Rs. 98 crore, directed with integrity and clinical focus, could have funded large multicentre randomised clinical trials of high-priority Ayurvedic interventions with existing preliminary evidence. It could have built GCP-compliant clinical research infrastructure within CCRAS institutes. It could have created a national Ayurveda research fellowship programme, training 200 to 300 physician-researchers in clinical trial methodology and biostatistics over five years — building the human capital this field desperately needs. It could have established a functioning pharmacovigilance network for Ayurvedic formulations — a genuine patient safety gap that currently goes largely unaddressed.

Instead, we have a cow urine distillate study with a synthetic contraceptive hormone in the data. We have a metabolomics paper that reported chemicals too heavy to pass through its own instrument. We have a materials science paper with energy storage efficiency above 100%.

These are not minor errors. These are not the inevitable imperfections of frontier science. These are the signatures of a research programme built on a political premise, producing data to match.

## Conclusion: Statistics Are Not Sacred Until They Are True

Prof. Mahalanobis gave India a statistical tradition grounded in the conviction that data must reflect reality — that numbers, carefully collected and rigorously analysed, can be the foundation of good policy and genuine progress.

On this National Statistics Day, I find myself reflecting on what it means when that tradition is inverted — when the conclusion is fixed in advance and the numbers are arranged to support it. When a synthetic contraceptive hormone appears in cow urine. When four samples report identical error values to three decimal places. When energy storage efficiency exceeds 100%.

These are not merely research failures. They are ethical failures. They represent a misuse of public trust, a waste of public funds, and a corruption of the scientific process that Ayurveda needs if it is ever to earn the international credibility it deserves.

I am asking these uncomfortable questions because I believe Ayurveda is worth fighting for — and fighting for means insisting on the truth, even when that truth is inconvenient, even when it implicates programmes launched in the name of our tradition, even when the institutions involved carry names that command great respect.

Numbers must be earned. They cannot be manufactured. And when they are manufactured — with public money, in the name of a healing tradition that hundreds of millions of people trust — the scholars, clinicians, and researchers of that tradition have not just the right but the responsibility to say so.
